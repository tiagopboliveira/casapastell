The Thesis

The Product

Pastel de nata. Multiple thin layers, hand-folded, baked at 500°F. The world's most perfect pastry — think of the combination between a flaky croissant and a creamy crème brûlée.

The Brand

Design-forward and built for social amplification. Electric blue packaging against a warm, minimalist interior — every cup, box, and bag is engineered to be photographed and shared.

The Timing

Americans have never been more aware of pastel de nata. Google search interest up 3x since 2018, social media conversations up 31% year-over-year.

Labor Model Notes

Industry benchmark: Labor costs at 25-35% of revenue for independent coffee shops (Taxfyle, Fresh Cup). Our model runs at ~35% of revenue including founder salary. Excluding the founder, staff-only labor is ~24% of revenue — in line with benchmarks.

D&A Assumptions: Equipment ($75,500) depreciated over 7 years. Leasehold improvements (construction + GC) depreciated over 10 years (lease term). D&A nets out in FCFF (subtracted for EBIT, added back for cash flow) but is shown for completeness. Capex: Maintenance capex at 2% of revenue covers ongoing equipment repair, minor upgrades, and operational upkeep. A major renovation (50% of initial buildout) is modeled in Year 5 to reflect the industry-standard refresh cycle for F&B retail. NWC: Modeled at 2.5% of revenue — minimal for a cash-based business with no receivables and perishable inventory (2–3 days of COGS). ΔNWC reflects the year-over-year change.

Why model taxes on an LLC? Casa Pastell will operate as an LLC, which means there is no entity-level tax — profits pass through to members who pay individual income tax. However, we apply a normalized 30% blended tax rate (Federal 21% + NY State ~6.5% + NYC UBT ~4%) for two reasons: (1) it makes returns directly comparable to any C-corp or franchise benchmark an investor might reference, and (2) it represents the economic tax burden that will ultimately be borne by the members, regardless of which entity pays. This produces a more conservative, apples-to-apples FCFF that does not overstate cash available for reinvestment or distribution.

A note on IRR: Returns are calculated using total invested capital (buildout + founder Year 0 compensation), a flat $150K/year founder salary, a normalized 30% tax rate, 2% maintenance capex, and a major renovation in Year 5. The terminal value uses a 5× EBITDA exit multiple, which is conservative for a branded F&B concept. The model assumes steady-state volume from Day 1 with no ramp-up period — a 6–12 month ramp to full volume would reduce Y1 EBITDA and lower the IRR by several percentage points.

Each new location follows the same trajectory as the 5-year pro-forma above: a store in its 1st year of operation uses Year 1 numbers, its 2nd year uses Year 2, and so on — matching the volume and price growth assumptions from the single-store model.

G&A (Corporate Overhead): Includes founder/CEO salary ($150K) plus corporate overhead: accounting, legal, insurance, and technology. From 5+ stores, includes operations management hires. G&A is not applicable for a single store (2027). Once the second location opens (2028), G&A grows sub-linearly relative to revenue — declining from ~8% (2 stores) to ~5% (15 stores) as overhead is spread across a larger store base.

Commissary Kitchen: At 5+ locations, we plan to evaluate a transition toward a centralized commissary kitchen model — consolidating production to improve unit economics (bulk purchasing, labor efficiency) while maintaining the exceptional product quality that defines the brand. Each store would continue to bake on-site from commissary-prepared dough and custard, preserving the fresh-from-the-oven experience.
